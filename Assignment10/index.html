Glacier National Park

Explore the untamed wilderness of Montana's crown jewel

Overview

Glacier National Park is a vast expanse of pristine wilderness located in the Rocky Mountains of northern Montana, bordering Canada. Known as the "Crown of the Continent," it boasts rugged peaks, crystal-clear lakes, ancient glaciers, and over 700 miles of hiking trails. It's a destination for adventurers, photographers, and nature lovers alike.

History

Glacier National Park was established in 1910 and has since become a symbol of America's natural heritage. The area has long been inhabited by Indigenous peoples, including the Blackfeet, Salish, and Kootenai tribes. Their presence and cultural heritage are still honored throughout the park today.

"Far away in northwestern Montana, hidden from view by clustering mountain peaks, lies an unmapped corner – the Crown of the Continent." – George Bird Grinnell

Wildlife & Ecosystems

The park is home to more than 70 species of mammals and nearly 300 species of birds. Grizzly bears, mountain lions, elk, mountain goats, and lynx roam the dense forests and alpine meadows. Diverse ecosystems thrive here, from cedar rainforests in the west to arid grasslands in the east.

Popular Attractions

  • Going-to-the-Sun Road: A 50-mile scenic drive crossing the Continental Divide with jaw-dropping views.
  • Lake McDonald: The park's largest lake, known for its vibrant rock colors and peaceful reflections.
  • Grinnell Glacier: A must-see glacier accessible by a strenuous but rewarding hike.
  • Many Glacier: A paradise for wildlife watchers and home to several stunning hikes.

Climate & Best Time to Visit

The park experiences long, snowy winters and short, mild summers. July through September are the most popular months to visit due to road access and warmer weather. Early summer still brings snow at higher elevations, while autumn offers colorful foliage and fewer crowds.

Tips for Visitors

  • Start your hikes early to avoid crowds and catch wildlife in the morning light.
  • Check the weather and trail conditions—many areas are inaccessible during late spring and early fall.
  • Bring bear spray and know how to use it. This is bear country!
  • Reserve lodging or campsites early—spots fill up fast during peak season.
